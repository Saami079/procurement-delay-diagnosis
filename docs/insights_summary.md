# Insights Summary

## Key Findings

### 1. Delays are Concentrated, Not Uniform
Delays are not evenly distributed across workflow stages.  
A small number of stages account for a large portion of total delay.

---

### 2. Bottlenecks are Stage-Specific
Certain approval stages appear more frequently as bottlenecks, indicating structural inefficiencies in the workflow.

---

### 3. Waiting Time is a Major Driver
In several stages, waiting time contributes more to delays than actual processing time.  
This suggests coordination and queue inefficiencies rather than workload alone.

---

### 4. Department-Level Variation Exists
Different departments exhibit different turnaround patterns, indicating operational variability.

---

### 5. Vendor Type Influences Delay
Requests involving external vendors tend to have higher turnaround times compared to internal processes.

---

### 6. Rework Significantly Increases Delay
Requests that undergo rework loops show higher stage times and contribute to workflow inefficiency.

---

### 7. Delay is Often Driven by a Single Stage
A large portion of total delay is often concentrated in one dominant stage, rather than being spread evenly.

---

## Modeling Insights

- Delay prediction is feasible using request-level features  
- Removing leakage features ensures realistic model performance  
- The model can identify high-risk requests with strong recall  

---

## Business Implications

- Focus improvement efforts on bottleneck stages  
- Reduce waiting time through better coordination  
- Monitor high-complexity and high-value requests  
- Track rework loops as a key inefficiency driver  

---

## Conclusion

Procurement delays are driven by identifiable process bottlenecks and operational factors, not randomness.

Targeted interventions at critical stages can significantly improve workflow efficiency.
